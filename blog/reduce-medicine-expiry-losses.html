How to Reduce Medicine Expiry Losses in a Pharmacy

Learn practical methods to identify short-dated medicines early, improve stock rotation, control purchasing and reduce avoidable expiry losses.

Medvika Healthcare Solutions • 21 July 2026 • 10 min read • 0 words
Last Updated: 21 July 2026
In This Guide

Table of Contents

Medicine expiry is one of the most preventable sources of financial loss in a pharmacy. Expired products consume working capital, occupy shelf space and create additional documentation and disposal work.

Expiry losses usually develop through repeated over-ordering, weak stock rotation, delayed reviews, poor demand forecasting and missed supplier return windows.

A pharmacy can reduce these losses significantly by identifying risk early and creating a disciplined process for purchasing, receiving, shelving, reviewing, returning and transferring stock.

01

Why Medicine Expiry Losses Happen

Expiry loss is often a symptom of a larger inventory-control problem. Understanding the cause is essential before deciding what action to take.

  • Purchasing excessive quantities to obtain discounts or schemes.
  • Ordering based on intuition instead of actual sales movement.
  • Keeping too many brands of the same slow-moving molecule.
  • Not checking expiry dates during receiving and shelving.
  • Failing to follow FEFO consistently.
  • Reviewing expiry only when products are already close to expiry.
  • Missing supplier return or replacement deadlines.
  • Continuing automatic reordering for slow-moving products.

Act early

The earlier a short-dated product is identified, the more options the pharmacy has: sell it, transfer it, return it, exchange it or stop further purchasing.

02

Measure Your Current Expiry Risk

Prepare a clear picture of existing expiry exposure by generating reports according to time remaining.

0–3 Months

Immediate action: stop repeat ordering and prioritize return, transfer or sale.

3–6 Months

High risk: review demand, supplier terms and stock movement.

6–9 Months

Early warning: adjust purchasing and monitor every month.

Value at Risk

Calculate the purchase value at risk, not only the number of products.

03

Follow FEFO Consistently

FEFO means First Expire, First Out. Products with the earliest expiry date should be sold or issued first.

  1. Receiving: Check batch and expiry before accepting stock.
  2. Shelving: Place earlier-expiry stock at the front.
  3. Billing: Select the correct batch in the software.
  4. Dispensing: Ensure staff do not pick newer stock first.
  5. Audit: Compare physical shelf arrangement with software records.

FEFO over FIFO

FIFO uses receipt date, while FEFO uses expiry date. In pharmacy inventory, expiry date should normally determine stock rotation.

04

Build a Monthly Expiry Review System

  • Generate a batch-wise expiry report on a fixed date each month.
  • Separate products into 0–3, 3–6 and 6–9 month groups.
  • Highlight high-value and high-quantity items.
  • Verify physical stock against software quantities.
  • Stop or reduce repeat ordering.
  • Identify return, transfer or exchange opportunities.
  • Assign each action to a responsible employee.
05

Improve Purchasing Control

The best way to reduce expiry loss is to prevent excessive stock from entering the pharmacy. Consider demand, current stock, supplier lead time, shelf life and working capital before ordering.

Avoid buying only for schemes

A discount does not create profit if the additional quantity remains unsold or expires.

Use reorder levels

Set minimum, maximum and reorder levels. Slow-moving or short-dated products should not remain on automatic reorder.

Review duplicate brands

Too many brands of the same molecule can divide demand and create slow-moving stock.

06

Identify and Control Slow-Moving Stock

Slow-moving products are future expiry risks. Generate reports for products with no sales or very low sales over 60, 90 or 180 days.

  • Stop repeat purchasing immediately.
  • Check whether another outlet has stronger demand.
  • Discuss exchange possibilities with the supplier.
  • Record why the product became slow-moving.
  • Prevent accidental reordering.
07

Use Supplier Return and Exchange Policies

Maintain clear records of eligible products, return windows, required documents and expected credit notes.

  • Ask for return terms before large purchases.
  • Maintain invoices and batch details accurately.
  • Prepare returns before the deadline.
  • Track pending credit notes and replacements.
  • Keep expired, returnable and saleable stock separated.
08

Transfer Stock Before It Becomes Critical

Multi-store businesses can move stock from a low-demand location to a location where the product sells faster. Transfers should be based on actual demand, remaining shelf life and applicable documentation requirements.

Transfer early

A product with six months of shelf life may still be useful elsewhere. The same product with only a few weeks remaining may be difficult to move.

09

Assign Clear Staff Responsibility

  • Train staff to read batch and expiry information correctly.
  • Include FEFO in written SOPs.
  • Define who generates reports and approves actions.
  • Review expiry performance in monthly meetings.
  • Record unresolved issues and deadlines.
10

Use Pharmacy Software and Alerts

Software can identify short-dated, slow-moving and non-moving stock, but only when batch and expiry information is entered accurately.

  • Batch-wise expiry tracking.
  • Alerts for products approaching expiry.
  • Slow-moving and non-moving reports.
  • Purchase restriction or reorder blocking.
  • Supplier return and credit-note tracking.
  • Stock transfer reports and adjustment audit trails.
11

Monthly Medicine Expiry Control Checklist

  1. Generate the full batch-wise expiry report.
  2. Review products expiring within 3, 6 and 9 months.
  3. Verify high-value items physically.
  4. Move earlier-expiry stock to the front.
  5. Stop repeat ordering for risky items.
  6. Prepare supplier returns and exchanges.
  7. Identify transfer opportunities.
  8. Review slow-moving and non-moving products.
  9. Track pending credit notes.
  10. Compare expiry value with the previous month.
12

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Checking expiry only once or twice a year.
  • Waiting until products are almost expired before acting.
  • Buying large quantities only for discounts.
  • Failing to stop automatic reordering.
  • Not reconciling supplier credit notes.
  • Allowing staff to pick newer batches first.
  • Depending entirely on software without physical verification.
13

Conclusion

Reducing medicine expiry losses requires early identification, disciplined purchasing, consistent FEFO, regular reporting and timely action. The process should begin months before the expiry date.

Pharmacies that combine accurate data, physical verification, staff accountability, supplier coordination and monthly review can protect working capital and improve inventory performance.

Quick Summary

Key Takeaways

✓

Identify expiry risk at least 3–9 months in advance.

✓

Follow FEFO during receiving, shelving and dispensing.

✓

Stop repeat ordering for slow-moving and short-dated products.

✓

Use supplier returns, exchanges and transfers before deadlines.

✓

Assign responsibility for expiry monitoring and follow-up.

✓

Track expiry value every month to measure improvement.
